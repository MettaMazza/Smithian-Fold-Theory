# Peer-review preparation

## Anticipated objections and honest responses

**"This is the doubling map re-dressed; the results are classical."**
The fold and the dyadic transformation are the same map; this is stated plainly in the paper, not avoided. Three theorems (R2, R4, RB1) correspond to established facts about that map; the other ten (R1, R3, R5–R10, RB2, RB3) are derived here and have no such correspondent in this form. The premise that overlap diminishes the work is not granted: a system derived from one axiom that meets established mathematics on common ground is, if its foundation holds, positioned to connect and unify existing results, not weakened by the contact. A reviewer who holds that a *specific further* result is already classical should name it by label, and it will be checked and either cited or distinguished — one result at a time, on evidence, not on the general impression that familiarity implies redundancy.

**"Removing negatives and imaginaries is a representation choice, not new mathematics."**
A serious objection. Response: the representation choice is the point, and it has a non-trivial consequence — that constants which appear as transcendentals in the trigonometric rendering (a band width 1/(2π) in an earlier complex model of this same system) appear as plain ratios ((m−1)/m) when the apparatus is removed, showing the transcendental was an artifact of the rendering rather than of the structure. That is a substantive observation about where π enters: the transcendental was a feature of the rendering, not of the structure. It is stated as a result, not offered for discount.

**"The emergence results are small and parameter-specific."**
E3, E4, E6 are labelled observations, not theorems, because they are measured at specific parameters; the distinction is made in the paper itself, not extracted by a reviewer. The emergence theorems (RB1, RB2, RB3) are exact and general. Observations are reported as observations and theorems as theorems; neither is dressed as the other.

**"The system claims to explain physics."**
It does not, and the paper says so explicitly in the discussion. No correspondence to physical phenomena is asserted or established. Any reviewer reading a physics claim into it has read more than is written.

**"How do we know the proofs are correct?"**
Every theorem has an exact rational confirmation; the full development reproduces from one command and emits a manifest mapping each theorem to its proof and confirmation. The language constraint is enforced by a static analyser verified to fail on a probe and to scan the whole corpus. The proofs are also given in full, so they can be checked by hand independently of the machine.

## Disclosure
The construction and verification used computational assistance (an AI system as an instrument under the author's direction, exact-arithmetic checking, and a static language gate). This is disclosed in the paper's appendix. The reproduction runner and the static gate let any reviewer re-derive and re-check every claim independently of that assistance.

## Venue
The work sits between formal systems, dynamical systems, and the foundations/history of mathematics. Candidate routes: a preprint on arXiv (plausible categories: math.DS dynamical systems; math.HO history and overview) to invite open comment, followed by submission to a journal whose scope includes either dynamical-systems expositions or foundational/heterodox formal systems. Framing must match the venue: a math.DS audience will expect the explicit relationship to the doubling map up front (Section 6); a foundations audience will weight the axiomatic and methodological angle. No claim is made here about acceptance — that is the reviewers' judgement.

## Contribution statement
We present a single-axiom system of positive-rational magnitude, with the dyadic fold as sole operation, in which thirteen theorems are proved from the One using ratio and division only — no negative, zero, or imaginary quantity — and three ensemble behaviours are measured. It is built in strictly positive-rational terms, its constants are ratios free of transcendentals, and its verification is mechanically enforced and reproducible from one command. The fold is the dyadic transformation, and the language is the Eudoxan theory of proportion; both are named and cited. The framework is intended as the basis of a unified physical theory; physical correspondences are pursued in a dedicated programme (Section 7 of the paper) and reported with an evidential tag — established, partial, or open — so the aim is stated in full while each result is claimed only to the extent it is shown.
